The Problem

The Hazard Was Known. The Communication Was Failing.

25
life-threatening sliding falls on Mount Washington per ski season, on average. Long sliding falls cause more fatalities on the mountain than avalanches and hypothermia combined.

The Mount Washington Avalanche Center had been communicating this hazard for years through trailhead signs, written forecasts, and direct visitor contact. Incident frequency had not declined. The existing communication approach was reaching users, but it was not changing behavior.

Field research and direct user observation surfaced the core problem: the target audience was not uninformed. They were underestimating. Experienced resort skiers were arriving with mental models of the terrain shaped by ski area experience, models that were fundamentally misaligned with the actual consequence environment at Tuckerman Ravine. They knew the mountain could be dangerous. They did not viscerally understand what a high-speed sliding fall on frozen snow looked and felt like.

"Year after year, we continue to see people make the same mistakes in the same terrain, and the consequences are often tragic and heartbreaking. Traditional warnings and trailhead conversations are not enough."

Two specific patterns from incident data made the problem concrete. First, the expert skier heuristic trap: serious falls frequently involved strong resort skiers whose skill and confidence were real, but whose mental model of consequence was calibrated for a managed ski area, not a 45-degree slope with a cliff band below it. Second, the timing problem: existing communication touchpoints were reaching users at the trailhead, after commitment and momentum had already taken over. The decision to go had already been made.

The Reframe

This Is a UX Problem, Not an Information Problem.

The standard response to a public safety communication failure is more communication: more signs, more warnings, more information. The diagnosis here was different.

The Design Reframe

The goal shifted from "inform visitors of the hazard" to "help visitors accurately model the consequence of a fall before they leave home."

That reframe changed everything: the format, the channel, the timing, and the content strategy.

If the problem is a mental model gap, the solution is not more text. Text cannot convey the physical reality of a high-speed sliding fall. The right medium can create visceral understanding in a way no sign or forecast ever could. Choosing the medium was the first and most consequential design decision.

Equally important was the timing insight. Intercepting users at the trailhead is too late. The right moment is during trip planning, when the decision is still open and the mental model can still be shaped. That meant distribution via YouTube, linked from avalanche forecasts, embedded in web content that targets the research phase of the user journey.

Design Decisions

Eight Choices and Why Each One Was Made

1
Film as the Primary Medium

Text cannot convey the physical reality of a high-speed sliding fall. A film can create visceral understanding in a way no sign or forecast ever could. The medium itself was a design decision, made before a single line of copy was written.

2
Intercept During Trip Planning

Distribution via YouTube and embedded links in avalanche forecasts targets users during the research and planning phase, before commitment has taken over. Reaching someone at the trailhead is too late. Reaching them on a Tuesday night while they plan their weekend is not.

3
Information Architecture for Multiple User Types

The page serves three distinct audiences without requiring any of them to wade through irrelevant content. Casual viewers arriving from YouTube get the hero, the film, and why it was made. Active trip planners find hazard science, warning signs, and mitigation guidance. Deep researchers get documented case studies with links to primary incident reports. Each layer is accessible and independently complete.

4
The "Not a Ski Area" Reframe

A key finding from user observation was that visitors were mentally categorizing Tuckerman Ravine as a ski destination rather than mountaineering terrain. A dedicated section directly challenges this mental model by naming the gap explicitly, not implying it, not hinting at it, but stating it plainly and explaining why it matters.

5
Naming the Expert Skier Heuristic Trap

Serious incidents frequently involve strong, experienced skiers. Calling this out by name, as a documented cognitive pattern rather than a judgment of individual competence, makes the content credible to exactly the audience most likely to dismiss it. Expert skiers do not respond well to being told they are not good enough. They do respond to being told that their expertise does not change the physics.

6
Responsibility Framing over Rules

Rather than telling users what to do, the design respects user autonomy while making the stakes of self-directed decision-making explicit. The hazard management section ends with turn-around triggers, not rules. The audience is experienced backcountry users. Telling them what to do would undermine credibility. Giving them a framework for their own decisions does not.

7
Emotional Resonance Through Real Stories

Three case studies drawn from primary MWAC incident reports ground the hazard in specific, documented reality. The 2024 fatality that catalyzed the entire project serves as the emotional anchor. Real names, real dates, real terrain. The choice to use actual incident data rather than hypothetical scenarios was deliberate: it makes the hazard real in a way that generalized warnings cannot.

8
Memorial as Closing Moment

The page closes with a tribute to those lost on the Presidential Range. This was not a standard content pattern for a public safety page. It was chosen because it gives the entire experience weight and purpose beyond information delivery. It makes clear that this is not a content marketing exercise. It is a response to real loss.

Measurement

The North Star Metric Is Behavior Change

The success of this project is not measured in pageviews. The page is designed to change what a user does on a mountain. That is a hard thing to directly attribute, and the design is honest about it.

Analytics Integration

PostHog analytics are integrated for scroll depth, section engagement, and click tracking. These proxies for engagement and comprehension are the closest available signals to actual behavior change at scale.

The film is available free, with no friction, no login, and no paywall. Reducing barriers to access was itself a design decision in service of the north star.

The project was executed at zero budget for web development. Every component, from the topo-map hero and editorial layout to the case study cards, pull quotes, and mobile navigation patterns, was designed and built directly in code without a design tool intermediary. The constraint was real; the output is live.
