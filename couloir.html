Checking the forecast is a five-tap problem.

Avalanche education is unambiguous on one point: check the forecast before you go, every time. The rule is not negotiable, and it is not complicated. The practice is less clean.

Eighteen regional avalanche centers cover the United States, each with its own website, URL structure, and forecast format. Getting today's danger rating for your specific zone means opening a browser, navigating to the right center, and finding the day's forecast page, usually in a dark parking lot before dawn, on cold hands, with a group waiting and headlamps already on heads. The friction is small. Small friction applied consistently to a safety behavior is how that behavior quietly erodes.

"The forecast is often published by the time you're heading out. The question is whether you actually look at it."

Common refrain in avalanche education

Couloir collapses the workflow to a glance. The current danger rating for any forecast zone in the US sits on your home screen, updates automatically, and taps through to the full forecast in one touch. The decision still belongs to the person going into the mountains. The information is just no longer three taps and a browser away.

Safety communication has an established visual language.

Every design decision in Couloir was made relative to one constraint: this is avalanche safety information, not a general-purpose weather app. The people who use it have spent time in avalanche education courses. They already know what the colors mean. The widget's job is to surface that information faithfully, not to reinvent the presentation.

NAPADS Fidelity

The North American Public Avalanche Danger Scale has official hex values, standardized iconography, and established terminology published by the National Avalanche Center. Couloir uses all of them verbatim: #50B848 for Low, #FFF200 for Moderate, #F7941E for Considerable, #ED1C24 for High, and #231F20 for Extreme. The danger icons on each widget tile are the official NAC rose icons, not custom illustrations.

A Detail That Matters

Moderate is the only danger level with a yellow (#FFF200) background, which requires black foreground text instead of white. This is specified in the official NAPADS color guidance and easy to overlook in implementation. Getting it wrong means reduced contrast on the one level where the color already demands the most careful treatment.

Smart Refresh Timing

WidgetKit widgets don't update continuously. They request a refresh on a schedule, and iOS decides when to actually execute it. The cadence has to be deliberate. Most avalanche centers publish their forecasts between 7 and 9 AM. Couloir requests a refresh every two hours during the 6–11 AM window, when a new forecast has likely dropped and you're making trip decisions. Outside that window, the interval extends to four hours. Forecasts don't change intraday. The expensive widget wake is concentrated where it earns its cost.

Widget Tap Behavior

Tapping either widget opens the full forecast for that zone in the browser, not just the app. The widget link resolves to the canonical forecast page for the selected center. For MWAC, the NAC API returns a generic link that doesn't deep-link to the Presidential Range forecast specifically. That case is caught at the model layer and overridden with the correct URL, so tapping the MWAC widget actually lands where it should.

Graceful Degradation

If the API is unreachable when the widget attempts a refresh, which is a reasonable thing to expect in a trailhead parking lot with two bars of LTE, the widget falls back to the last-cached forecast. The cached danger rating and travel advice are still displayed, along with the time it was last updated. A forecast from three hours ago is still decision-relevant. A blank widget at 6 AM is not.

The Medium Widget's Travel Advice

The wider medium widget has room for a second column, and the obvious choice would be to display the zone-specific advisory text from the API. The problem is that zone-specific text is written for reading, not scanning. It varies in length, changes its level of detail inconsistently between centers, and doesn't fit predictably in a fixed widget area.

Instead, the medium widget shows the canonical NAPADS travel advice for the current danger level. This text is short by design, written to be scanned, and consistent across every level. "Very dangerous avalanche conditions. Travel in avalanche terrain not recommended." Eight words and a period. That's what High means, in the same language used in every avalanche education program in the country. The full zone-specific advisory is one tap away.

Build details.

Platform iOS 17+ (required for AppIntentConfiguration in WidgetKit)
Framework SwiftUI for both the companion app and widget views. WidgetKit for the widget extension. AppIntents for interactive widget configuration.
Data Source National Avalanche Center public API at api.avalanche.org/v2/public. GeoJSON map-layer endpoint returns all active forecast zones with current danger levels, travel advice, and forecast timestamps.
Data Sharing App Groups (group.com.couloir.shared) via UserDefaults. The ForecastStore actor manages saved zones and cached forecast data accessible to both the companion app and widget extension.
Widget Sizes Small (2×2) Medium (4×2)
Configuration SelectZoneIntent (AppIntent) + AvalancheZoneEntity + AvalancheZoneQuery. Zones populated from saved favorites in the companion app, with fallback to a full center fetch for first-time configuration.
Refresh 2-hour interval during 6–11 AM. 4-hour interval all other hours. Controlled via WidgetKit's Timeline policy with a computed nextUpdate date.
Coverage 18 US avalanche centers: BTAC, CAIC, CBAC, CNFAIC, ESAC, FAC, GNFAC, IPAC, KPAC, MSAC, MWAC, NWAC, PAC, SAC, SNFAC, UAC, WAC, WCMAC.
Assets Official NAPADS danger rose icons from the National Avalanche Center, adapted for light and dark rendering. Danger-level colors per the NAC published hex specification.

Platform constraints, honestly stated.

Couloir was built for a specific use case on a specific platform. The limitations below are direct consequences of its architecture, not oversights.

01
iOS Only
WidgetKit is an Apple framework. Android users cannot install this widget. Building a cross-platform equivalent would require a fundamentally different architecture, likely a web-based widget or a separate native build.
02
US Centers Only
Coverage is limited to the 18 centers in the NAC public API. Canadian, European, and other international avalanche forecasting systems use different APIs and different danger scale conventions. Adding them would require additional API clients and careful handling of non-NAPADS scale variants.
03
No Hazard-Change Notifications
WidgetKit widgets pull data on a schedule; they cannot push a notification when a danger level changes. A significant overnight upgrade, say Considerable to High after new storm snow, will not alert you. The widget reflects the current rating whenever iOS refreshes it. Monitoring for changes and alerting would require a different delivery mechanism entirely.
04
Companion App Required
The widget depends on the companion app to save zones before configuration is possible. A bare widget installation, without ever opening the app, presents only a "Select a Zone" placeholder. The companion app doubles as a full zone browser and forecast reader, so this is a useful relationship, but it is a dependency.
The Project

Built for the backcountry.

Couloir is a personal tool, built because the thing didn't exist, distributed with a step-by-step Xcode setup guide so anyone can run it on their own device. No App Store account required.
