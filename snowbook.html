Snowbook

Product Pitch
A four-minute walkthrough of the snowbook concept.

4 min  ·  snowbook product pitch  ·  Patrick Scanlan

Avalanche forecasters are making critical decisions on incomplete data.

Data collection in mountain environments is genuinely hard. Access is remote, power sources are often nonexistent, and the terrain that matters most is exactly the terrain that is most difficult to instrument. In winter, this data scarcity means avalanche forecasters and snow scientists are left to rely on inconsistent field observations, weather model outputs, and a sparse network of fixed weather stations to make snowpack assumptions across enormous, variable terrain.

The consequences of that gap are significant. Forecast accuracy depends directly on the spatial density of snowpack data. Where data is thin, uncertainty is high. And where uncertainty is high, the margin for error in hazard communication narrows.

More data points, better spatial awareness, and consistent delivery from locations no fixed station will ever reach.

snowbook was designed to close that gap directly: a network of low-cost, deployable snow sensors that can be placed anywhere, run on battery power for a month at a time, and transmit measurements automatically every 30 minutes without requiring field personnel to be present.

Designed by a forecaster, tested with forecasters.

Prototype development focused on deliberate simplicity. Rather than building a comprehensive weather station, the design isolated two measurements: snow depth and temperature. That constraint kept unit cost low, kept power demands within battery range, and kept the product focused on the data that contributes most directly to avalanche hazard assessment. Adding sensors was always possible. The question was whether adding them was justified at this stage, and early on the answer was no.

Hardware design sketch, 2023. Internal components (top) and field deployment configuration (bottom).

User research was conducted with professional avalanche forecasters through a structured beta testing protocol. The primary finding shaped subsequent product decisions: the critical adoption requirement was not sensor accuracy or battery life, but integration with existing forecasting workflows and databases. snowbook addresses this by treating the existing SNOTEL and fixed station network as a collaborator rather than a competitor. The machine learning model is designed to ingest existing infrastructure data alongside snowbook sensor readings, making the product additive to the current system rather than a replacement.

Three audiences, one platform.

snowbook is designed around three distinct professional use cases, each of which benefits from the same core capability: reliable, high-resolution snowpack data from locations that were previously unmeasured.

01
Professional Avalanche Forecasters
Deploy sensors across forecast zones to increase spatial resolution of snowpack data. Improved data density feeds directly into hazard rating accuracy and the quality of public safety communication.
02
Research Scientists
Instrument remote terrain for climate, meteorology, and hydrology research. snowbook enables consistent, longitudinal data collection in locations previously accessible only through expensive and time-intensive manual measurements.
03
Ski Resorts
Gain higher-fidelity understanding of snowpack conditions across ski area terrain and assess the effectiveness of avalanche mitigation efforts with real measurement data rather than estimates.

Deploy, measure, transmit, predict.

snowbook units are installed by field technicians across locations where snowpack data is needed. From that point, the system operates autonomously. Snow depth and temperature measurements are collected continuously and transmitted to a central database via SMS every 30 minutes, with no requirement for local power infrastructure or network connectivity beyond a cellular signal. The beta version transmits via SMS, with satellite connectivity on the roadmap for locations beyond cellular coverage.

Battery life supports approximately 30 days of continuous operation before a technician returns to swap the battery or collect the unit. That runtime is long enough to capture the arc of a storm cycle and its aftermath without mid-deployment intervention.

On the software side, incoming sensor data flows into two output streams. Each unit reports independently to the cloud without communicating with other units in the network, which means any number of sensors can be added or removed without affecting the rest of the system. Raw measurements are made available directly for specialist use. In parallel, the data feeds snowbook's machine learning model, which combines sensor inputs with standard geo-relevant datasets to generate high-integrity predictions of avalanche hazard and snowpack structure.

Thirteen inputs. Three elevation bands. One coherent forecast.

The predictive model at snowbook's core is an XGBoost regression model trained on MWAC weather and hazard data from the 2022–23 season. Thirteen features feed the model, each chosen to capture a distinct dimension of snowpack state. Several required deliberate engineering decisions before they could be used effectively.

Wind direction is a circular variable — 359° and 1° are nearly identical in the real world, but numerically they sit at opposite ends of a range. A naive encoding would introduce false distance into the model. To address this, wind direction is decomposed into sine and cosine components, preserving the true angular relationship between readings.

Seasonal timing is encoded as a continuous elapsed-time variable combining month and day into a single fractional value. This gives the model a smooth, unbroken representation of where in the season a given reading falls, rather than treating month as an arbitrary integer.

Total snow depth accounts for nearly 50% of predictive weight — more than all other features combined.

Feature importance analysis reveals a clear hierarchy: total snowpack depth (hs) accounts for 49.9% of the model's predictive weight, elapsed time through the season at 14.5%, and new storm snow at 10.4%. Wind speed, temperature, and sky conditions contribute meaningful but secondary signal. The implication is significant — the single most reliable predictor of hazard is how much snow has accumulated over the season, not what the weather is doing on any given day.

Three-Band Elevation Output

Producing separate predictions for three elevation bands is a deliberate design choice, not a technical convenience. Avalanche hazard is fundamentally elevation-dependent — a day with Considerable hazard above treeline can carry Low hazard at the base. Forecasters communicate hazard by elevation band for precisely this reason, and a single composite score would discard the spatial structure that makes ratings actionable in the field.

What sets snowbook apart from fixed weather stations.

01
Spatial Resolution
More data points across variable terrain produces fundamentally better spatial awareness of snowpack conditions than a fixed network ever can.
02
Cost
Traditional fixed weather stations, with sensors, data loggers, solar arrays, and installation, cost over $100,000 per site. A snowbook unit costs $116 in components. That cost difference is not just a price story: it is what makes spatial density financially viable for the first time. The value of the product is not in replacing one high-accuracy data point, but in placing many affordable ones across terrain that would never support permanent infrastructure.
03
Consistency
Automated 30-minute transmission cycles deliver data continuously, without the gaps that come with resource-intensive and weather-dependent manual measurements.
04
AI-Powered Prediction
The XGBoost model connects field measurements to hazard ratings and snowpack structure predictions, bridging raw sensor data and actionable safety intelligence.
