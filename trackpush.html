Guiding someone out of the wilderness over the phone.

When a hiker is lost, injured, or disoriented in the backcountry, search and rescue coordinators face a frustrating communication gap. They know the terrain, they know the egress route, and they have the caller on the line, but transferring that spatial knowledge verbally is slow, error-prone, and fails entirely when the caller is panicked or unfamiliar with map reading.

The alternatives require app installs, account creation, or expensive satellite hardware. None of that works when a lost person is standing in the woods with a stressed mind and 18% battery.

"The route is always in my head. The problem is getting it into theirs."

SAR Coordinator, White Mountains

TrackPush was built to close that gap. A SAR professional draws an egress route and sends it as a link. The recipient taps it, grants location permission, and a live navigation interface appears in their browser. No app store. No account. No setup.

Six steps from distress call to navigation.

01
Locate the Caller
SAR receives GPS coordinates from 911 dispatch, a cell ping, or the caller's description. The coordinator opens TrackPush and finds the position on the topo map.
02
Draw the Egress Route
The coordinator taps waypoints on the map to define the path to safety. GPX files from existing tracks can also be uploaded and simplified automatically.
03
Generate the Link
One tap produces a shareable URL. The entire route is polyline-encoded directly into the URL hash, requiring no server, no database, and no account.
04
Send It
The link goes out via SMS, iMessage, email, or through a satellite messenger relay. Any channel works. The link is short enough to read back verbally if needed.
05
Recipient Opens It
The caller taps the link. Their browser opens Follow mode: no app download, no account prompt, no delay. They grant location access in one tap.
06
They Walk. The App Guides.
Live GPS position, a compass bearing to the next waypoint, distance remaining, and a progress bar. The interface is designed for cold hands, low battery, and high stress.

Every choice in service of one user state.

The follow mode interface is designed around a person who is cold, scared, and probably has less than 30% battery. That framing informed every decision from color palette to interaction model.

Serverless by Design

The entire route is encoded into the URL itself using Google's polyline encoding algorithm. There is no backend, no database, no API key required. The app works from a static file host, reduces latency to near zero, and eliminates any failure surface in the link-sharing pipeline. The tradeoff is URL length at high waypoint counts, which is mitigated by a GPX simplification algorithm that reduces tracks to a configurable maximum of 50 points.

Technical Approach

Routes are encoded using the Polyline encoding algorithm, compressing lat/lng pairs into a compact ASCII string. The full route decodes in the browser on load, requiring no network request beyond the initial page and map tiles.

High-Contrast Dark UI

Follow mode uses a dark theme not for aesthetics but for outdoor legibility. High-contrast orange and teal on near-black surfaces remain readable in direct sunlight and reduce the screen brightness needed in low-light situations, extending battery life.

Compass Heading Logic

The directional arrow pulls from two sources, depending on conditions. When the device is moving faster than 0.5 m/s, GPS velocity heading is used as it is more accurate than the magnetometer in motion. When stationary, the device compass (with appropriate handling for the iOS permission model and Android's deviceorientationabsolute event) takes over. The result is a heading arrow that behaves correctly in both scenarios without user configuration.

Waypoint Progression

The app automatically advances the active waypoint when the user comes within 30 meters of it, updating the bearing and distance readouts to point toward the next target. This removes the cognitive load of manually tracking progress through the route.

Build details.

Stack Vanilla HTML, CSS, JavaScript. Leaflet.js for mapping. OpenTopoMap and OpenStreetMap tile layers.
GPS & Sensors Geolocation API (watchPosition), DeviceOrientationEvent with iOS 13+ permission handling and Android deviceorientationabsolute.
Routing Manual waypoint placement, GPX file import with Douglas-Peucker-style simplification, URL-encoded polyline for link generation.
Geocoding Nominatim (OpenStreetMap) for place search. Coordinate parsing supports decimal degrees and DMS.
Hosting Static. No server required. Deployable to any CDN or file host.
Platforms iOS Safari Android Chrome Firefox Mobile Desktop
Dependencies Leaflet 1.9.4. No other runtime dependencies.

What TrackPush does not do (yet).

Honest accounting of constraints is part of good product design. TrackPush was built for a specific, well-defined use case, and the limitations below are direct consequences of its architecture.

01
Requires Cell Service
Map tiles load over the network. The app works where the caller has a signal. Offline tile caching is on the roadmap but not yet implemented.
02
One-Way Only
The serverless architecture means SAR cannot see the caller's real-time position within the app. The route is pushed; progress is not reported back. Two-way tracking would require a backend, which changes the deployment model significantly.
03
Battery Consumption
Continuous GPS and screen-on usage will drain a phone. Best suited for situations where sufficient charge remains to complete the egress. Screen dimming and background GPS are potential future optimizations.
04
URL Length at Scale
Dense GPX tracks with thousands of points exceed practical URL lengths. The built-in simplification algorithm addresses this, but very long routes require a tradeoff in precision.
